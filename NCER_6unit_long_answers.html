Unit 1
What is a solar cell? Explain its working principle with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
A solar cell (photovoltaic cell) is a semiconductor device that converts sunlight directly into DC electricity using the photovoltaic effect — no moving parts, no fuel, no emissions.
2Construction
  • Top layer: n-type silicon (extra electrons)
  • Bottom layer: p-type silicon (extra holes)
  • p-n junction forms at the boundary — creates a built-in electric field
  • Anti-reflection coating on top to absorb max light
  • Metal contacts (silver grid on top, aluminium on back) to collect current
Solar cell — cross section diagram
3Working principle (step-by-step)
  • Photons from sunlight strike the n-type layer
  • Photons with energy ≥ band gap excite electrons → generate electron-hole pairs
  • Built-in electric field at p-n junction separates charges: electrons go to n-side, holes to p-side
  • Electrons flow through external circuit → electric current (DC) is produced
  • This is called the photovoltaic (PV) effect
4Key parameters
  • Voc = open circuit voltage (~0.5–0.6 V per cell)
  • Isc = short circuit current
  • Fill Factor (FF) = measure of quality
  • Efficiency = 15–22% (commercial mono-Si)
5Materials used
  • Mono-crystalline silicon — highest efficiency
  • Poly-crystalline silicon — lower cost
  • Thin film (CdTe, CIGS, Amorphous Si) — flexible, low cost
6Advantages & limitations
  • Advantages: no fuel, no emissions, long life (25+ years), silent
  • Limitations: works only in daylight, high initial cost, efficiency drops in heat
Exam tip: Always draw the cross-section showing n-type (top), p-n junction (dashed), p-type (bottom), external load circuit. Label: anti-reflection coating, metal contacts, electron flow direction.
Unit 1
Explain photovoltaic (PV) water pumping system with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
A PV water pumping system uses solar panels to generate DC electricity which drives an electric motor-pump to lift or transfer water — used for irrigation, drinking water in rural/remote areas.
PV water pumping system — block diagram
2Main components
  • PV array — converts sunlight to DC electricity
  • PCU (Power Conditioning Unit) / MPPT — optimizes and converts power (DC to AC if needed)
  • Electric motor — AC or DC type
  • Centrifugal pump — lifts water from source
  • Water storage tank — stores pumped water for use at night
  • Sensors and controls — for automatic ON/OFF
3Working
  • Sunlight falls on PV array → DC electricity generated
  • PCU controls voltage and converts DC to AC (if AC motor used)
  • Motor converts electrical energy to mechanical energy
  • Pump is driven by motor → water is lifted from well/reservoir to tank
  • System works only during daylight hours (no battery needed if tank is large enough)
4Types
  • Surface pump — water source near surface (≤ 7m)
  • Submersible pump — motor and pump submerged in deep well
5Advantages
  • No fuel cost, zero emissions
  • Ideal for remote, off-grid areas
  • Low maintenance, long life
  • KUSUM scheme (India) subsidizes these systems
6Limitations
  • Works only in daytime / sufficient sunlight
  • High initial cost
  • Output varies with cloud cover
Exam tip: Draw block diagram with 5 boxes: PV Array → PCU → Motor → Pump → Tank. Draw arrows showing energy/water flow. Label each component's function below.
Unit 2
Explain solar flat plate collector as solar air heater with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
A flat plate collector (FPC) is a device that absorbs solar radiation and transfers the heat to a working fluid (air or water). In solar air heaters, air is the working fluid — no pumping liquid required.
Solar flat plate collector (air heater) — cross section
2Main parts
  • Transparent cover (glass): allows solar radiation in, reduces convection heat loss (greenhouse effect)
  • Absorber plate: blackened metal (copper/aluminium) — absorbs max solar radiation
  • Air channel / duct: space where cold air enters and hot air exits
  • Insulation: glass wool / rock wool at bottom and sides — reduces heat loss
  • Metal casing: structural support frame
3Working (step-by-step)
  • Solar radiation passes through the glass cover
  • Blackened absorber plate absorbs radiation → temperature rises
  • Cold air enters from one end of the air channel
  • Air gets heated by convection from the hot absorber plate
  • Hot air exits from other end and is used for application
  • Glass cover traps heat inside (like a greenhouse)
4Performance factors
  • Solar intensity and collector tilt angle
  • Absorptivity of absorber plate (should be close to 1)
  • Air flow rate through the channel
  • Quality and thickness of insulation
  • Number of glass covers (double glass = less heat loss)
5Applications
  • Crop drying (paddy, spices, fruits)
  • Space heating in buildings
  • Industrial process heat (low temperature)
  • Timber drying, textile drying
6Advantages & limitations
  • Advantages: simple, no moving parts, low maintenance, cheap
  • Limitations: works only in daytime, low temperature range (40–80°C), needs large area
Exam tip: Draw cross-section clearly. Show 5 layers: glass → absorber (black) → air channel → insulation → casing. Draw arrows for cold air in (left), hot air out (right), sun rays coming from top.
Unit 2
Explain: Solar constant, Solar spectrum, Clarity index, Declination angle, Zenith angle, Day length hours.
6 marks
1Solar constant (Isc)
Average solar radiation received per unit area at Earth's mean orbital distance from the sun = 1367 W/m². It remains nearly constant throughout the year (varies ±3% due to Earth's elliptical orbit).
2Solar spectrum
Distribution of solar energy with wavelength. Three regions: UV (<400 nm) = 9%, Visible (400–700 nm) = 47%, Infrared (>700 nm) = 44%. Peak emission near 500 nm (green light).
3Clarity index (Kt)
Ratio of global horizontal solar radiation (H) to extraterrestrial radiation (H₀) on horizontal surface. Kt = H / H₀. Value 0 to 1. Higher Kt = clearer sky = more solar energy received at ground.
4Declination angle (δ)
Angle between the sun's rays and the equatorial plane. Ranges from +23.45° (June 21, summer solstice) to −23.45° (Dec 21, winter solstice). Formula: δ = 23.45 × sin[360/365 × (284 + n)]°, where n = day number.
5Zenith angle (θz)
Angle between the direction of the sun and the vertical (directly overhead = zenith). θz = 0° means sun is directly overhead. θz = 90° means sun is at horizon (sunrise/sunset). cos θz = sin φ sin δ + cos φ cos δ cos ω, where φ = latitude, ω = hour angle.
6Day length (N)
Duration of sunshine from sunrise to sunset. Formula: N = (2/15) × cos⁻¹(−tan φ × tan δ) hours. On equinox days N = 12 hours everywhere. Longer in summer, shorter in winter (in higher latitudes).
Exam tip: This is a 6-mark definitions question — write 1 short definition + formula for each term. Draw a simple earth-sun diagram showing declination angle and zenith angle on it.
Unit 3
Derive power developed by wind. Define Betz limit. Explain HAWT with sketch.
6 marks
1Power in wind — derivation
  • Consider air of density ρ (kg/m³), velocity V (m/s), swept area A (m²)
  • Mass flow rate: ṁ = ρ × A × V (kg/s)
  • Kinetic energy of wind = ½ × ṁ × V² = ½ × ρAV × V²
  • Power available in wind: P = ½ ρ A V³ (watts)
  • Key insight: P ∝ V³ — doubling wind speed → 8× more power
  • P ∝ A — larger rotor = more power
2Betz limit
A wind turbine cannot extract 100% of wind energy — air must keep moving to leave the rotor area. Maximum theoretical efficiency = 16/27 ≈ 59.3% (Betz limit). Achieved when downstream velocity = V/3. Practical turbines achieve 35–45%.
Horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT) — labeled diagram
3Key wind machine parameters
  • Cut-in speed: minimum wind speed to start generating power (~3–4 m/s)
  • Rated speed: wind speed at which rated (maximum) power is produced (~12–15 m/s)
  • Cut-out speed: wind speed at which turbine is shut down for safety (~25 m/s)
  • Tip speed ratio (λ): ratio of blade tip speed to wind speed. Optimal λ = 6–8 for HAWT
4Types of rotors
  • HAWT (Horizontal axis): blades rotate in vertical plane, axis horizontal. Most common (3-blade propeller type)
  • VAWT (Vertical axis): Savonius, Darrieus types. Axis vertical. Work in any wind direction
  • HAWT advantages: higher efficiency, better at extracting energy
5Site selection for wind farm
  • Average wind speed ≥ 6 m/s annually
  • Open terrain — coastal, hills, plains
  • Away from buildings and obstructions
  • India: Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, Rajasthan are top wind states
Exam tip: Write P = ½ρAV³ formula clearly in derivation. Betz limit = 59.3% must be mentioned. Draw 3-blade HAWT with nacelle, hub, tower, foundation labelled.
Unit 3
What is OTEC? Explain open cycle OTEC with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
OTEC (Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion) is a system that generates electricity using the temperature difference between warm surface ocean water (~25–28°C) and cold deep ocean water (~4–5°C). Minimum ΔT required = 20°C.
Open cycle OTEC — schematic
2Open cycle OTEC — working steps
  • Step 1: Warm surface seawater (~26°C) is pumped into a vacuum evaporator (low pressure chamber)
  • Step 2: At low pressure, water flash-evaporates into low-pressure steam
  • Step 3: Steam drives a low-pressure turbine which is connected to a generator → electricity produced
  • Step 4: Exhaust steam enters a condenser
  • Step 5: Cold deep ocean water (~4°C, pumped from 1000m depth) condenses the steam
  • Step 6: Condensed water = fresh (desalinated) water — useful byproduct!
3Closed cycle OTEC (brief)
Uses a working fluid like ammonia (low boiling point). Warm water vaporizes ammonia → drives turbine → cold water condenses ammonia back → cycle repeats. Seawater is not the working fluid. More efficient than open cycle.
4Advantages
  • Renewable, 24×7 operation (ocean temperature is stable)
  • Open cycle produces desalinated water as byproduct
  • Zero fuel cost, near-zero emissions
  • Suitable for tropical coastal regions (India, Pacific islands)
5Limitations
  • Very low thermal efficiency (~2–3%)
  • Very high capital cost (deep water pipe is very expensive)
  • Suitable only near tropics where ΔT ≥ 20°C
  • Environmental concerns: cold water upwelling disturbs marine life
Exam tip: Draw 4 boxes: Warm water → Evaporator → Turbine+Generator → Condenser → desalinated water out. Cold water pipe from 1000m depth going to condenser. Label ΔT = 20°C minimum.
Unit 4
What is geothermal energy? Explain its types and working with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
Geothermal energy is heat energy stored in the Earth's interior — in its core, mantle, and crust. Sources of this heat: (a) radioactive decay of elements (uranium, thorium, potassium) and (b) residual heat from Earth's formation. Temperature increases with depth at ~30°C per km (geothermal gradient).
Geothermal flash steam power plant — diagram
2Types of geothermal systems
  • Dry steam: Direct steam from underground → turbine. Oldest type. (Geysers, California)
  • Flash steam: Hot pressurized water (>180°C) pumped up → pressure drop → flashes to steam → turbine. Most common.
  • Binary cycle: Moderate temp water (~100–180°C) heats secondary fluid (isobutane/ammonia) → secondary fluid vaporizes → turbine. Used for lower temperature resources.
  • EGS (Enhanced Geothermal System): Water injected into hot dry rock → heated → extracted. Man-made reservoir.
3Flash steam working (most common — above diagram)
  • Hot pressurized water drilled from depth (~1–3 km)
  • Water enters flash separator → pressure drops → flashes to steam
  • Steam drives turbine → generator produces electricity
  • Exhaust steam condensed → water reinjected into ground (sustainable)
4Geothermal heat pump
Uses shallow ground temperature (~15°C constant year-round) to heat buildings in winter (ground is warmer than air) and cool in summer (ground is cooler than air). No electricity generation — direct heating/cooling.
5Advantages
  • Reliable 24×7 — not dependent on sun or wind
  • Very low CO₂ emissions
  • Small land footprint
  • Can provide both electricity and direct heating
6Limitations
  • Site-specific — not available everywhere
  • High drilling costs
  • H₂S gas release can cause pollution
  • Risk of induced seismic activity (EGS)
Exam tip: Draw flash steam plant — production well → flash separator → turbine+generator → condenser → injection well. Label hot water in, steam path, power out, and water reinjection.
Unit 4
What is biomass energy? Explain biogas production process with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Biomass definition
Biomass is organic matter from living or recently living organisms — plants, animals, agricultural waste, forestry residue, and municipal solid waste. It stores solar energy in chemical form (through photosynthesis). It is renewable as long as plants keep growing.
2Types of biomass feedstocks
  • Agricultural waste: paddy straw, sugarcane bagasse, corn stover
  • Energy crops: jatropha, switch grass, miscanthus
  • Wood and forestry residue: sawdust, wood chips
  • Animal waste: cow dung, poultry manure
  • Municipal solid waste (MSW): organic fraction of city garbage
3Biogas — definition & composition
Biogas is produced by anaerobic digestion (breakdown of organic matter without oxygen). Composition: 55–70% methane (CH₄) + 30–40% CO₂ + traces of H₂S. Calorific value: ~20–25 MJ/m³. It is a renewable substitute for natural gas (LPG).
Floating drum biogas plant (KVIC type) — diagram
4Anaerobic digestion — 4 stages
  • Hydrolysis: Complex molecules (proteins, fats, carbohydrates) broken into simpler ones (sugars, amino acids)
  • Acidogenesis: Bacteria convert to volatile fatty acids + CO₂ + H₂
  • Acetogenesis: Fatty acids converted to acetic acid, H₂, CO₂
  • Methanogenesis: Methanogens convert acetic acid + H₂ → CH₄ (methane = biogas)
5Advantages of biogas
  • Renewable and carbon-neutral
  • Reduces methane emissions from waste (greenhouse gas control)
  • Digestate (slurry) is excellent organic fertilizer
  • Decentralized energy for rural areas (cooking, lighting)
  • Reduces dependence on LPG/firewood
6Limitations
  • H₂S content causes corrosion in pipes and engines
  • Requires continuous feedstock supply
  • Lower gas yield in cold climates (bacteria need 25–37°C)
  • Large space required for community plants
Exam tip: Draw floating drum plant — show underground digester, floating drum on top, inlet (slurry), outlet (digestate), and biogas pipe leading to burner. Label the 4 digestion stages as a flow.
Unit 5
What is a fuel cell? Explain design and operating principle with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
A fuel cell is an electrochemical device that converts chemical energy of a fuel (hydrogen) directly into electrical energy without combustion. It is like a battery but never runs out as long as fuel is supplied. No moving parts, no noise, no emissions (only water as byproduct).
Hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell (PEMFC type) — diagram
2Reactions (easy to remember)
  • At Anode (−): H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ (hydrogen gives up electrons)
  • H⁺ ions pass through electrolyte to cathode
  • Electrons flow through external circuit → create electric current
  • At Cathode (+): ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O (water forms)
  • Net: H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O + electricity + heat
3Types of fuel cells
  • PEMFC (Polymer Electrolyte): temp ~80°C, used in vehicles (Toyota Mirai)
  • AFC (Alkaline): KOH electrolyte, used in NASA spacecraft
  • PAFC (Phosphoric Acid): temp ~200°C, commercial buildings
  • MCFC (Molten Carbonate): temp ~650°C, uses natural gas
  • SOFC (Solid Oxide): temp ~1000°C, highest efficiency (~60%)
4Polarization losses (problems)
  • Activation loss: energy needed to start the reaction
  • Ohmic loss: resistance of electrolyte and contacts
  • Concentration loss: fuel/oxidant starves at high current density
5Advantages & limitations
  • Advantages: high efficiency (40–60%), zero direct emissions, silent, no moving parts
  • Limitations: expensive platinum catalyst, H₂ storage is difficult, high cost
Exam tip: Draw anode | electrolyte | cathode vertically. Label H₂ input at anode, O₂ at cathode. Show H⁺ ions through electrolyte and e⁻ through external circuit to load. Write both half reactions.
Unit 5
Explain working of Lithium-ion battery with neat sketch.
6 marks
1Definition
A lithium-ion (Li-ion) battery is a secondary (rechargeable) battery that stores energy by moving lithium ions between anode and cathode through an electrolyte. It is the most widely used battery in mobile phones, EVs, and laptops today.
Lithium-ion battery — working diagram
2Construction
  • Anode (−): Graphite (carbon layers where Li⁺ ions are stored during charging)
  • Cathode (+): Lithium metal oxide — LiCoO₂, LiFePO₄, or LiMnO₂
  • Electrolyte: Lithium salt (LiPF₆) dissolved in organic solvent — conducts Li⁺ ions
  • Separator: Porous polyethylene/polypropylene membrane — prevents short circuit
3Working — discharge (battery powering device)
  • Li⁺ ions leave graphite anode → travel through electrolyte → insert into cathode
  • Electrons flow from anode → external circuit → cathode (creates current)
  • At cathode: Li⁺ + e⁻ + cathode → LiCoO₂ (lithium inserts)
4Working — charging (plugged in)
  • External voltage forces Li⁺ to leave cathode → travel through electrolyte → insert back into graphite anode
  • Electrons forced through external circuit from cathode to anode
  • Reverse of discharge process
5Characteristics
  • Cell voltage: ~3.6 V (much higher than Pb-acid 2V or Ni-MH 1.2V)
  • Energy density: 150–250 Wh/kg (best among commercial batteries)
  • Cycle life: 500–2000 cycles
  • No memory effect
6Advantages & limitations
  • Advantages: high energy density, lightweight, long cycle life, no memory effect
  • Limitations: expensive, thermal runaway risk if overcharged, requires BMS (battery management system)
Exam tip: Draw anode (graphite) | electrolyte | cathode (LiCoO₂). Show Li⁺ moving left (discharge) and right (charge). Draw e⁻ flow through external circuit with a load/bulb symbol.
Unit 6
Discuss operating principle of MHD power generation with neat sketch. What are its challenges?
6 marks
1Definition
MHD (Magnetohydrodynamic) power generation produces electricity directly from a hot conducting fluid (ionized gas/plasma) moving through a magnetic field — based on Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction, applied to a fluid conductor. No turbine, no moving mechanical parts.
MHD power generation — principle diagram
2Working principle (step-by-step)
  • Fuel (coal/gas) is burned at very high temperature (~2500°C)
  • Seed material (potassium carbonate K₂CO₃) is added to improve electrical conductivity of gas
  • Hot ionized gas (plasma) flows at high velocity through the MHD duct
  • A strong magnetic field (B) is applied perpendicular to the flow
  • Lorentz force (F = qv × B) acts on ions → positive ions go to one electrode, negative to other
  • Potential difference created across electrodes → DC electricity collected
3Advantages of MHD
  • No moving parts → no mechanical wear
  • Very high theoretical efficiency (50–60%) when combined with steam cycle
  • Can use various fuels (coal, gas, nuclear)
  • Quick start-up and shutdown
4Challenges / limitations
  • Requires very high temperature (>2000°C) for plasma ionization — difficult to achieve and maintain
  • Electrode erosion due to hot corrosive plasma
  • Seed material (K) recovery is complex and expensive
  • Needs very powerful superconducting magnets (expensive)
  • Low conversion efficiency standalone — must combine with conventional steam cycle
  • Still mostly at research/experimental stage worldwide
5Applications
  • Used as a topping cycle (before steam turbine) to increase overall plant efficiency
  • Space power generation (nuclear-powered MHD)
  • Military propulsion (electromagnetic torpedoes)
Exam tip: Draw duct with plasma flowing horizontally. Magnetic field arrows coming from top (N pole). Electrodes on left and right walls. Current to external load. Label: v (velocity), B (magnetic field), F (Lorentz force). Write F = qv × B.
Unit 6
Describe flywheel and supercapacitor as energy storage. Compare both.
6 marks
1Flywheel — definition & construction
A flywheel stores energy as rotational kinetic energy in a spinning rotor. Construction: massive disc/cylinder of steel or carbon-fibre composite, mounted on a shaft with magnetic bearings (frictionless), enclosed in vacuum housing to reduce air drag.
Flywheel energy storage — diagram
2Flywheel working
  • Charging: Electric motor spins the flywheel to high RPM (10,000–100,000 rpm) → stores kinetic energy
  • Discharging: Flywheel slows down and drives a generator → releases electrical energy
  • Motor and generator are often the same machine (motor-generator)
  • Vacuum enclosure reduces friction losses; magnetic bearings eliminate contact friction
3Supercapacitor — definition & construction
A supercapacitor (electric double-layer capacitor, EDLC) stores energy electrostatically in an electric double layer at the electrode-electrolyte interface. No chemical reaction — purely physical charge separation. Construction: two porous carbon electrodes (very high surface area) separated by an electrolyte and a porous separator.
4Supercapacitor working
  • When charged: ions from electrolyte accumulate at electrode surface (double layer forms) → electric field stores energy
  • When discharged: ions return to electrolyte → energy released as current
  • Key: no chemical reaction → extremely fast charge/discharge (seconds) and very long life
  • Energy = ½CV² (C = capacitance, up to thousands of farads)
5Comparison table
  • Energy density — Flywheel: 5–100 Wh/kg · Supercapacitor: 1–10 Wh/kg
  • Power density — Flywheel: high · Supercapacitor: very high (1–10 kW/kg)
  • Cycle life — Flywheel: millions · Supercapacitor: >500,000 cycles
  • Self discharge — Flywheel: ~10–20%/hour · Supercapacitor: ~10–40%/day
  • Response time — Both: milliseconds (very fast)
  • Applications — Flywheel: UPS, grid stabilization · Supercapacitor: hybrid vehicles, burst power electronics
6Advantages & limitations
  • Both: much longer life than batteries, fast response, no chemical degradation
  • Flywheel limitation: gyroscopic effects, safety (rotor burst), high cost for carbon fibre
  • Supercapacitor limitation: high self-discharge, low energy density (poor for long-term storage)
Exam tip: For flywheel, draw disc + shaft + magnetic bearings. Write E = ½Iω². For supercapacitor, draw two carbon electrodes with + / − ion layers at surface. Key comparison: supercapacitor has higher power but lower energy density than flywheel.
